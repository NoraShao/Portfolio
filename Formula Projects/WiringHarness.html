FSAE Car Wiring Harness

September 2023 - April 2024

The wiring harness in our race car consists of three main harnesses; the main trunk harness, engine harness, and the sensor harness.
The main trunk and engine harness work together to provide power to and enable communication between all the electrical modules on the car. The main trunk harness connects the main electrical modules at the front of the car, and connects to the engine harness at the back of the car via an engine bulkhead connector. Like its name suggests, the engine harness connects to the car engine and delivers signals to various modules at the back of the car, including for shifting and actuating the brake light. The harnesses are essential for the operating of the car.
The sensor harness primarily aids data collection on various parts of the car while its running, which other subteams use to improve their designs. The signals from the sensors (i.e. brake pressure, wheel speeds, shock position, etc.) all travel to our AIM EVO5 Datalogger, where we collect the data from, via a USB or wifi.

Main Trunk Harness

  • Provides power to and enables communication between the primary electrical modules (i.e. Power Distribution Module, Engine Control Unit, Shifting Module, Steering Wheel, Dash)
  • Connects signals and power frome the modules at the front of the car to modules at the rear of the car (where the engine harness is located) via an engine bulkhead connector

Sensor Harness

  • Primarily aids data collection on various parts of the car while its running, which other subteams use to improve their designs.
  • Signals from the sensors (i.e. brake pressure, wheel speeds, shock position, etc.) all travel to two connectors on our AIM EVO5 Datalogger, where we collect the data via a USB or wifi.
  • Lower priority for me than the main trunk harness since its not necessary for running and testing the car

In the 2023-2024 school year, I was responsible for remaking the main trunk and sensor harnesses. Because the sensor harness is not essential for the car to run, we prioritized designing and manufacturing the main trunk harness.

Harness Design

I started designing the main trunk and sensor harnesses by mapping out the necessary signals between all the modules in Google Sheets. The primary goal of doing this was to have an accessible and easily updatable record of what signals each wire from each connector socket carried. Most of the connectors and connections were of key electrical modules that didn't change from year to year, but I also had to figure out how to incorporate changes for the new year such as new electrical modules and different signals that subteams needed.

For organization and safety, we followed these guidelines for selecting wires:

  • 22 AWG wires for power wires not expected to carry excessive current and all signal wires
    • 5V (sensor) power: red
    • General signals: blue
    • Sensor GND: purple
    • CAN HI, CAN LO (for CAN bus communication between modules): yellow, green
  • 18 AWG wires for power lines expected to carry more current. The higher wire gauge is to minimize the risk of overheating.
    • 12V power: orange
    • Power GND: black

To develop a more accurate visualization and record of the harness encapsulating its physical layout as well, we used a harness design software, RapidHarness.

Harness Manufacturing

To main the robustness of both harnesses, we made sure they were protected against any environmental or factors or risks related to being in an automotive. For example, they needed to safely operate on the car in any weather condition, and shouldn't be damaged by the movement of the car. To ensure this, we follow these specifications when manufacturing them:

  • Used Tefzel wires, which are used in many motorsport and industrical applications due to their chemical resistance and high temperature tolerance.
  • Created relief loops at the base of all wires protruding from connectors, and secured them with thermally stable and electrically insulating Kapton tape: this relieves stress from the crimp pins securing the wire in the connector sockets when wires are pulled, and also offers leeway in terms of wire length if we need to cut and repin the other side of the wire.
  • IP-65 rating or higher for all connectors: This ensures the junctions between the wires and electrical modules should be highly dust and water resistant, preventing faulty signal connections or dangerous water shorts. We primarily use Deutsch DTM (for sensors and general purpose), AMPSeal, and on out-sourced modules, OEM connectors, and cover the OEM connectors with heat shrink boots.
  • Enclosed all wire bundles and connectors with Raychem DR-25 heatshrink: DR-25 heatshrink is laceration-resistant, which protects the wires from being cut by factors such as excessive rubbing against sharp edges (which occurs frequently due to the aggressive movement of the car). It also protects the wires from possible water shorts.
  • Concentric-twisted all wire bundles: This serves two purposes. First, twisting wires increases their relative angles, minimizing electrical noise from the induced magnetic fields around each current-carrying wire. Second, twisting wires makes them more resist to stress from bending, because instead of wires being either fully compresses or stretched depending on their position in the bundle, twisted wires will be compressed and stretched at different points.
In addition to manufacturing the brake light PCB, I was also responsible for protecting it from environmental factors and mounting it on the car. I designed a 3D-printable resin enclosure in Solidworks that could be mounted onto the rear differentials of the car and would also act as a diffuser for the LEDs. Before I secured the PCB in its water and airtight enclosure, for additional water resistance, I applied a layer of conformal coating onto the PCB itself.
